If you ever became interesting in productivity, then maybe you have heard the word system as well, specially if you read the book [Atomic Habits](https://www.goodreads.com/en/book/show/40121378-atomic-habits).

If not, it is a way that you do something, like task management or habit tracking; some people keep todo lists, some people like [Eisenhower Matrix](https://www.todoist.com/pt-BR/productivity-methods/eisenhower-matrix) and some people like [kanbans](https://www.atlassian.com/agile/kanban).

In my quest for being better at what I do, I have found that systems are, particulary more complex then "check the box", at least if you want to keep it long term and not abandon it after a couple weeks.

# Rules for Systems

I'm no researcher, productivity guru or bilionaire, but these are the things I think make a good system:

- it must have a purpose
- it must be sustainable
- and it must be resilient

The rest of the post will be my explanations on why.

## It Must have a Purpose

If a kid asks you "why" and your response is "because I said so", don't be surprised he will have a grudge on you and the task is done in a poor way.

Humans are creatures of purpose, nothing you ever do comes because "uuh, I don't know, I just feel like I should". 

There's must always a reason we do something or behave, wether we know it or not, and systems being tools by humans reflect that. 

From my limited experience of this world, some of the unhappiest and most non fulfilling jobs in this world come from people who are distant from their results, aka, devlopers, office jobs, marketing... In other words, people who "don't see a purpose".

If you have a system merely for the sake of it, like a morning routine "because it's productive", soon it will be a chore and another thing to siphon your energy you could be using to something better.

## It Must be Sustainable

It doesn't matter how good your habits are and how well thought is your routine or method to do anything if it always demands your 110% to do it. Eventually the rock will stop rolling and you will need a lot of strength to get it up rolling again, or give up (the usual case for me).

As a means of example, say you always go 110% in your running, you feel pumping and heart beating, all good vibes from the runner's high, and don't even mention the sunshine and rainbows.

But then, something happens!! And now, your baseline, your "average" in everything goes from from 80-100% to 70-50%. That's almost half in the worst case, half of what you are capable off. But given that you "conditioned" yourself to thinking that, it's only good if you give your 110%, you will work at your 50% as if you was in your 100% trying to go 200%. 

The math may not math here, but the point is, you need to not exagerate, you need to go a bit lower then **you think** you can. Not because you **cannot**, but because eventually the friction will be too high to continue.

## It Must be Resilient

And at last, this one is connected to the previous, so much so it was a bit hard to find how they differentiate.

Look, you can say to me an entire dissertation about how discipline and "doing the hard work even when you don't feel like" is better then relying on motivation and I could agree with you, but there's some grey between that white and black.

Again, sometimes we condition ourselves into doing more then we can.

The one thing I have found to combat this friction, is saying to myself "at the very least".

With this I mean, "at the very least, run 2km", "at the very least, open the file", "at the very least, go outside".

Even if the ammount that I do is pathetic, even if I do 5 reps, 3 reps or heck, 1 rep, it's better then 0. Because I have done.

This example will be kinda weird, but recently, I simply didn't want to eat a avocado, and if you ever bought one, you know that once open, it will start to rapidly go dark, and after that, only a matter of time before you find it less appealing and it rapidly rots. And yet, I said to myself "I'll eat this, even if it's only 1 spoon, even if it is 1 teaspoon, but I'll". I did, not just a spoon, but the entire slice I cut.

To summarize, and kinda quote a certain character from a movie about cars:

> Sometimes you gotta go down to go up

# Ending Thoughts

I call this, for now, "rationalization", and is based on the ideia that, apparently when you intentionally think about a emotion you are having, it starts to have less effect on you. Can't confirm if it is or if this ideia is true, but seems related to that.
